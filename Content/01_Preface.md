# Preface

Welcome to the Exercise Book for the course **AE2135-I Aircraft Structural Analysis and Design**. This book contains homework exercises, design challenges, and practice exam questions for students to use in their learning journey. The aim is to co-create this exercise book with students, so if while using it you have an idea for how to add to it or improve it, dont hesitate to communicate your suggestion in the Exercise Book Discussion Forum in the course Learning Management System (BrightSpace).

## Approach to solving problems in this book
One of the most common mistakes students make when solving engineering problems is rushing directly toward an answer. In doing so, they often neglect the most important part of engineering practice: clearly communicating their understanding of the problem, the assumptions they make, the simplifications they introduce, and the reasoning that connects one step to the next.

In engineering practice, a correct numerical answer alone has limited value if nobody can understand how it was obtained. Aircraft structures are analysed and designed by teams of engineers, meaning that your work must be transparent, traceable, and verifiable by others. For this reason, the quality of the reasoning and communication is often more important than the final answer itself.

### The IDEA approach
Throughout this book, it is recommended that problems are approached in a structured and systematic way. A useful framework for this is the IDEA approach:

```{figure} ./Figures/IDEA.svg
IDEA framework (Interpret, Develop, Evaluate, & Assess), adapted from https://freekpols.github.io/Mechanica/content/classic/language/ 
```
::::{tab-set}
:::{tab-item} Interpret
> **Interpret** - Understand the problem before writing equations. 

Ask yourself what is the system, what behaviour is important, and what is being asked? Communicate your understanding of the problem by **always making a clear sketch**. A good sketch clarifies geometry, loads, supports, coordinate systems, variables, datums, and assumptions. 


:::
:::{tab-item} Develop
> **Develop** - Construct an appropriate model of the problem. 

Identify the governing physical phenomena and formulate the governing equations and boundary conditions needed to solve for the desired unknowns. Make sure to establish and setup all of the relevant equations before you get lost in solving them!

:::
:::{tab-item} Evaluate

> **Evaluate** - Solve the model.

Carry out the mathematical analysis in a clear and organized manner, ensuring that each step follows logically from the assumptions and model development.

:::
:::{tab-item} Assess

> **Assess** - Critically examine the result.

Do the units make sense? Is the magnitude reasonable? Does the result agree with physical intuition and expected behaviour? **A solution is not complete until it has been questioned!**

:::

::::

Following this approach aligns with the goals of this course to not not merely train students to obtain answers, but to help them develop disciplined engineering thinking: interpreting problems carefully, modelling them thoughtfully, solving them systematically, and assessing results critically.

### Additional tips for approaching problems
- **Practice solving problems using paper and pen.** While digital tools can make drawing figures and writing equations faster, they can also reduce practice in clearly communicating solutions by hand. Since assessments are typically completed using paper and pen, it is important to regularly practice organizing and presenting your work in this format.

- **Know when to use computational tools.** For homework and self-study, using computers, programming, or programmable calculators is encouraged where appropriate. These tools allow you to focus on understanding modelling and analysis rather than repetitive arithmetic. Examination problems, however, are designed with the limitations of a basic scientific calculator in mind.

- **Learning occurs during struggle.** Difficulty solving a problem is not a sign of failure; it is often the point at which learning occurs. When stuck, try to identify what concept, assumption, or insight is missing. This helps direct further study, guides useful questions in class or discussion forums, and highlights opportunities to learn collaboratively with others.

## Exercise types explained
This book contains more exercises then you are expected to complete over the duration of the course, and contains different exercise types that serve different learning purposes. The various types of exercises are briefly described below to help you decide how best to use this book.

### Homework Exercises
These are the core exercises intended for all students taking the course. They are designed to challenge your understanding of the material through realistic and moderately complex problems.

The questions are intentionally not simplified to minimize arithmetic or algebraic effort. As a result, they require careful and rigorous solution procedures, including the ability to trace and identify errors within your own analysis. For these exercises, the use of programmable calculators or computers is encouraged to assist with repetitive or complex calculations.

Only final answers are provided. Developing the full solution process independently is considered an essential part of the learning experience.

### Design Challenges
Design challenges are open-ended problems intended to resemble real engineering decision-making more closely.

These exercises typically do not have a single correct answer. Instead, they require you to apply course concepts to justify assumptions, compare alternatives, and support design decisions. The goal is to encourage discussion, critical thinking, and engineering judgement rather than arriving at one “correct” numerical result.

### Sample Exam Questions
These questions are designed to reflect the style and constraints of examination problems.

Compared to the homework exercises, they are generally more structured and simplified. Intermediate steps, partial solutions, or reduced algebraic complexity may be provided to limit the effect of carry-through errors during assessment.

These problems are best used to evaluate your mastery of the concepts after working through the more challenging homework exercises. They should ideally be solved using paper and pen with only a basic scientific calculator.
